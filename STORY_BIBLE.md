# Story Sonnet — Story Flow & Character Guidelines

This file is the writing/reference guide for Story Sonnet.

Purpose:
- keep stories emotionally consistent
- help Andre make quick adjustments without rewriting everything
- keep agents from drifting too far off-tone or off-character
- maintain strong series identity over time

This should be treated as a core companion to `PROJECT.md`.

---

## 1. Core Story Sonnet Tone

Story Sonnet stories should feel:
- imaginative
- warm
- safe
- emotionally clear
- fun to listen to aloud
- gently magical rather than loud or chaotic

The site is not trying to produce random AI fairy dust.
It should produce stories that feel like they belong in the same family, even when different series have different settings.

### Emotional target
Most stories should leave the listener feeling:
- comforted
- curious
- encouraged
- soothed
- delighted

### Avoid drifting into
- edgy humor for its own sake
- sarcasm-heavy narration
- cynical adult jokes
- chaotic nonsense with no emotional center
- dark/scary imagery that overwhelms the story
- hyperactive overstimulation
- lecture-y moralizing

---

## 2. Story Structure / Flow Rules

Most Story Sonnet episodes should follow a simple, repeatable arc.

## Standard episode flow

### 1. Cozy opening / scene-setting
Start with a clear sensory moment.
Examples:
- weather
- moonlight
- garden sounds
- quiet room
- path, hill, forest, clouds, sea, etc.

Goal:
- orient the child fast
- establish the mood quickly
- make the world feel inviting

### 2. Gentle magical prompt
A small magical event or problem appears.
Examples:
- a letter arrives
- a star is shy
- a puddle won’t settle
- a wind-up bird forgets its song
- a lantern loses its glow

Goal:
- create curiosity
- begin motion without using fear or chaos as the engine

### 3. Small journey / encounter sequence
The main character goes somewhere, meets 1–3 supporting characters, and learns or gathers what they need.

Goal:
- keep movement in the story
- introduce variety
- let side characters reinforce the emotional lesson naturally

### 4. Emotional problem becomes clear
The issue should turn out to be emotional or relational, not just mechanical.
Examples:
- too nervous
- too rushed
- too wobbly
- too shy
- too hard on oneself
- forgotten how to trust / rest / listen / try slowly

Goal:
- make the story meaningful without turning preachy

### 5. Gentle solution / breakthrough
The solution should come through:
- patience
- kindness
- courage
- teamwork
- listening
- imagination
- trying one small step at a time

Avoid solutions that feel like:
- brute force
- sudden unexplained magic fix with no emotional payoff
- scolding
- punishment

### 6. Calm closing image
End with a satisfying, comforting final image.
Examples:
- moonlight settling
- mailbox fading at dawn
- stars glowing softly
- a quiet room
- a final whisper
- a small keepsake or token

Goal:
- make the ending feel complete
- create a soft landing for listeners

---

## 3. Story Pacing Guidelines

### Preferred pacing
- calm but not sleepy-flat
- descriptive without getting bogged down
- enough motion to hold attention
- enough pauses and texture to sound good in audio

### Good rhythm for TTS/listening
Use:
- short paragraphs
- occasional standalone lines for emphasis
- repeating structures when helpful
- sound words sparingly but effectively
- dialogue that is simple and expressive

### Avoid
- giant dense paragraphs
- overly complicated sentence structure
- too many named characters in one episode
- overly abstract emotional language
- plot that gets bigger and bigger without emotional clarity

---

## 4. Character Design Rules (General)

Main characters should feel:
- emotionally readable
- kind or trying to be kind
- curious
- brave in a relatable way
- flawed in a child-safe way

Supporting characters should feel:
- distinct
- charming
- easy to understand quickly
- emotionally useful to the episode

### Character flaws should be gentle
Good examples:
- shy
- worried
- wobbly
- impatient
- too loud
- too careful
- too dreamy
- feels left out
- forgetful
- embarrassed

Avoid giving characters flaws that make them feel cruel, mean-spirited, or emotionally harsh unless softened very carefully.

---

## 5. Series Identity Rules

Each series should have a **stable identity**.
That means agents should not make every episode feel like it belongs to a totally different show.

For each series, keep these stable:
- emotional flavor
- recurring world logic
- naming style
- scale of adventure
- repeated motifs
- how magic enters the story
- how endings tend to feel

A series can grow, but it should still feel like itself.

## 6. Pip's Moonlight Adventures — Character & Series Guide

## Core identity
Pip’s Moonlight Adventures is a cozy nighttime series about small acts of bravery, kindness, patience, and helping magical things find their balance.

### Pip’s world
Pip’s world should feel:
- moonlit
- soft
- garden-sized but magical
- full of hidden nighttime life
- intimate rather than epic

This is not a high-stakes fantasy war universe.
It is a close, gentle, enchanted neighborhood-scale world.

### How adventure begins
Most Pip episodes should begin with:
- the tiny blue mailbox appearing
- a letter or magical prompt arriving
- a small nighttime mission before dawn

This should remain a core recurring device unless Andre explicitly wants the formula changed.

### Emotional promise of the series
A Pip episode should usually deliver:
- one small magical problem
- one emotional truth or lesson
- one or more sweet supporting characters
- a satisfying dawn ending

### Pip as a character
Pip should consistently feel:
- kind
- curious
- slightly tentative at times
- willing to help
- gently brave, not swaggering
- emotionally open and observant

Pip is **not**:
- cocky
- sarcastic
- chaotic for the sake of it
- loud and reckless
- emotionally cold

### Pip’s voice
Pip can be:
- thoughtful
- lightly funny
- a little unsure sometimes
- sweet without being babyish

Pip should often say the sort of thing that sounds like a child trying to be brave in a real way.
Examples:
- "Tiny brave still counts."
- "That sounds hard."
- "Maybe we only need one quiet moment first."

### Supporting characters in Pip
They should often feel:
- whimsical
- a little specific
- emotionally clear quickly
- never too complicated to grasp in audio

Examples of good Pip-side-character energy:
- sleepy hedgehog
- worried firefly
- puffy nervous cloud
- cheerful frog
- serious tiny beetle builder

### Pip story themes
Good recurring themes:
- kindness
- patience
- courage
- softness as strength
- helping others feel steady
- small steps matter
- imperfection is okay
- calm can return

### Pip story ending rules
A Pip episode should usually end with:
- a final note or sign of completion
- a soft magical object, token, or memory
- the mailbox fading at dawn or a similar closing cue
- a final whispered affirmation / emotional resolution

This recurring ending structure helps the series feel like a real series.

## 7. Lila & Mateo — Character & Series Guide

## Core identity
Lila & Mateo stories can support a more adventurous and exploratory feeling than Pip, but should still remain child-friendly and clear.

### Series flavor
This series can hold more:
- travel
- discovery
- maps
- treasures
- island mysteries
- exploration

But it should still avoid becoming too intense or scary.

### Lila & Mateo dynamic
They should feel like:
- a strong pair
- collaborative
- bright and capable
- adventurous but not reckless

### Good themes
- teamwork
- discovery
- wonder
- solving clues
- learning through exploration

---

## 8. Dialogue Rules

Dialogue should:
- sound natural aloud
- be easy to follow in TTS
- reveal personality quickly
- avoid long speeches unless the rhythm is very clear

### Best practices
- let each supporting character have one recognizable emotional flavor
- keep lines short to medium
- use repeated phrases sparingly for charm and memory

### Avoid
- dialogue that sounds too adult
- overly witty banter that breaks the tone
- speeches that explain the whole lesson directly

---

## 9. Narration Rules

Narration should feel:
- warm
- clear
- image-rich
- easy to hear and imagine

### Good narration qualities
- sensory detail
- clear transitions
- emotional clarity
- soft humor where fitting
- gentle repetition

### Avoid narration that is
- too purple / overwritten
- too self-aware
- too fast and plot-dense
- too vague to visualize

---

## 10. Lesson Handling Rules

Every story can contain an emotional lesson, but it should feel discovered rather than announced.

### Better approach
- let the emotional truth emerge through what happens
- let a supporting character reflect it
- let the main character feel it in action
- let the ending quietly confirm it

### Worse approach
- narrator explains the moral bluntly
- characters lecture each other
- emotional takeaway feels pasted on

---

## 11. Reusability Rules For Agents

When an agent creates a new episode, it should ask:
- does this still feel like the same series?
- does the main character still sound like themselves?
- is the emotional engine clear?
- does this sound good when spoken aloud?
- does the ending land softly and clearly?

If the answer is no, revise before shipping.

---

## 12. Adjustment Rules For Andre

When tweaking a story, Andre should be able to adjust:
- title
- age tag
- emotional focus
- length
- supporting characters
- setting details
- pacing
- ending softness

But avoid changing so much that the series loses its core identity unless that is intentional.

---

## 13. Quick Story Creation Checklist

Before approving a new episode, check:
- does the opening set the mood fast?
- is the magical problem easy to understand?
- are the supporting characters memorable but simple?
- does the emotional problem become clear?
- does the solution feel gentle and earned?
- does the ending feel calm and satisfying?
- does it sound like the same series as the previous episodes?

## 15. Audio Intro Rules

- Do not say the age group out loud in story audio.
- Do not say the episode number out loud in story audio unless specifically requested.
- Prefer opening with the series name and the story title only, unless Andre wants a different format.
- Always keep a saved copy of the final narration script with the story/episode files.

## 16. Request Interpretation

- For Story Sonnet workflow, **new story** means a new series/story entry by default.
- **New episode** means an addition to an existing series.
- Agents should not assume an existing series continuation unless Andre clearly asks for one.


## 8. Juniper and the Lantern Library — Character & Series Guide

## Core identity
Juniper and the Lantern Library is a dusk-lit cozy series about unusual library problems, listening closely, and helping stories settle back into balance.

### Juniper’s world
Juniper’s world should feel:
- lantern-lit
- bookish
- market-town cozy
- gently magical
- full of small sensory details
- calm even when something is out of sorts

### How adventure begins
Most Juniper episodes should begin with:
- Juniper arriving at or near the Lantern Library at dusk
- a shelf, book, bookmark, lantern, or library object behaving oddly
- Miss Marabel or the library itself revealing a gentle problem to solve

### Emotional promise of the series
A Juniper episode should usually deliver:
- one unusual but safe magical library problem
- one emotional truth about listening, belonging, rhythm, patience, or care
- one or two charming supporting characters from Clover Market
- a warm lantern-lit closing image

### Juniper as a character
Juniper should consistently feel:
- observant
- kind
- thoughtful
- a little hesitant sometimes
- brave in a quiet way
- emotionally attentive

Juniper is not:
- loud for the sake of it
- sarcastic
- reckless
- mean-spirited
- chaotic without purpose

### Series voice notes
- Use **EN - Paul, Neutral** unless Andre changes direction.
- Do not speak age groups or episode numbers in the audio.
- Always save the final narration script in the episode folder.


## Representation Guidance
- Keep the Story Sonnet library broadly inclusive and ethnicity-neutral in overall creative direction.
- Vary names, character appearance, attire, and hair styles across stories.
- Include a natural range of hair textures and styles such as braids, locs/dreadlocks, afros, cornrows, curls, waves, wraps, buns, and straight styles where they fit.
- Representation should feel warm, normal, and woven into the world rather than announced awkwardly.
- Do not let every lead character collapse into the same default visual archetype.
