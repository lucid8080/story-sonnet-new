# The Keepers of Turtleshell City

## Episode 1
**Title:** The Bell Beneath the Lantern Streets
**Voice:** EN - Jane, Curious
**Length Target:** ~11 minutes

Far above the fields, above the rivers, above the winding roads where caravans looked no bigger than breadcrumbs, a city was walking.

It did not roll on wheels.
It did not float on clouds.
It walked on four slow, pillar-strong legs.

Because the city stood on the shell of a gigantic turtle.

The turtle's name was Mossback Morning.

From far away, Mossback Morning looked like a green hill with lanterns on top.
From a little closer, he looked like a whole neighborhood drifting gently through the world.
And from the streets of the city itself, he felt like home.

There were crooked little houses with painted shutters. There were rooftop gardens full of mint and beans and bright marigolds. There were rain barrels, wind-catchers, laundry lines, soup stalls, and round stone plazas where musicians played in the evening while the city swayed in the calm rhythm of giant footsteps.

Everyone in Turtleshell City knew an important truth.

A city on a turtle did not stay safe by accident.

Pipes had to be cleared.
Lantern rails had to be repaired.
Water tanks had to stay balanced.
Bridges between shell-ridges had to be checked after every long walk.
And most important of all, Mossback Morning himself had to be cared for with great gentleness and great respect.

That was why Turtleshell City had its maintenance crew.

They called themselves the Keepers.

And on the morning this story begins, the Keepers were having breakfast on the east overlook while the whole city crossed a meadow sparkling with dew.

Tavi stirred cinnamon oats in a tin pot and peered over the railing with bright, thoughtful eyes.
Tavi wore a saffron work scarf, sturdy patch-pocket overalls, and their dark curls were gathered into two puff-braids to keep the wind out.

Beside them sat Imani, who could hear a strange rattle in a pipe from three alleyways away.
Imani had warm brown skin, a coil of copper tools at one hip, and long locs wrapped today in a blue patterned band.

Jun sat cross-legged on an upside-down crate with a maintenance tablet balanced on one knee. Jun's straight black hair was tucked behind one ear, except for the piece that never obeyed, and they wore a moss-green jacket with shiny brass toggles.

Suri leaned against a railing post, chewing pear slices and watching the clouds. Suri liked wind-maps, pulley systems, and impossible knots. Her dark hair was braided into one thick rope with little red thread woven through it.

And Niko, the youngest full Keeper, was trying to butter toast while holding a wrench, which everyone had already told him was an unnecessary risk.
Niko had a tumble of chestnut curls, a grin that arrived before he did, and exactly the sort of energy that made breakfast on railings seem reasonable.

"I am being careful," Niko announced, as the toast slid off the knife and landed butter-side down on his boot.

"You are being memorable," said Imani.

Tavi tried not to laugh and failed.

Below them, the meadow bent in waves under Mossback Morning's slow steps.
Each footstep sent the gentlest shiver through the stones beneath the city. Bowls trembled. Lantern chains chimed. Somewhere down Lantern Market, a baker probably muttered, There goes the cinnamon topping again.

Jun glanced at the morning board.
"Today's route is simple," they said. "Check north gutter runnels, oil the west bridge hinges, polish the sun mirrors, and do the shell-song health round before supper."

At that, everyone paused for a tiny respectful moment.

The shell-song health round was the most important task of all.
Once each day, the Keepers listened through special brass listening tubes set into the turtle's shell ring.
The shell carried tiny echoes from the massive living body below: the low rush of breath, the deep slow thrum of the great heart, the shifting creak of muscles, the sleepy gurgle of a very large stomach digesting clover, pondweed, and apples from friendly farms.

If the echoes sounded calm, Mossback Morning was well.
If they sounded odd, the Keepers investigated at once.

"After breakfast," said Tavi, "we'll do the shell-song first. I had a dream last night that the city was humming in the wrong key."

Niko blinked.
"Was that a real warning dream or a cheese-dream?"

"Hard to say," said Tavi. "There was also a singing turnip."

Imani stood and hooked her tool coil at her belt.
"Either way, we listen early."

So they packed up the oat pot, rescued Niko's toast from his boot, and headed across the lantern streets.

Turtleshell City was waking around them.
A girl with silver beads in her cornrows swept her family's flower stall.
An elder in a plum shawl adjusted tiny paper pinwheels outside a tea cart.
Two siblings raced past carrying a basket of socks and laughing so hard they nearly forgot to stop at the bridge rail.
A tailor with a soft gray afro pinned bright patches to rain capes on a clothesline that fluttered like flags.

Everywhere, the city glowed with small useful things lovingly tended.
That was the Keepers' favorite kind of beauty.

The shell-song posts stood in a circle near the city's center, where the shell rose into a broad mossy ridge shaped like an old green hill.
Five brass tubes curved out from the stones there, each tipped with a listening cup.
When the Keepers pressed an ear to them, they could hear Mossback Morning's hidden music.

They took their places.

Tavi listened first, then frowned.

"Do you hear that?" they whispered.

Imani took the next tube.
Jun took another.
Suri and Niko leaned in too.

Usually the shell-song sounded like this: breath, heart, step, hush.
Breath, heart, step, hush.
Slow and certain as dawn.

Today, woven beneath it all, was another sound.

Dong.

A deep, gentle bell.

Then silence.

Then, after a long pause—

Dong.

Niko straightened so fast he bonked the back of his head on Suri's elbow.
"Ow. Also, that was definitely a bell."

"There are no bells under the shell," Jun said.

"There should not be," Imani corrected, which was how she said something felt serious.

Tavi listened again. The bell was not loud. It was not panicked. But it did not belong.
And stranger still, each time it rang, the shell-song underneath grew slightly slower.

Breath... heart... step... hush.
Dong.
Breath... heart... step... hush.

Suri looked toward the road ahead, where the meadow dipped into a dry golden plain.
"He's slowing down," she said.

All across the city, people were beginning to notice.
Laundry lines swayed less.
The tea in a vendor's kettle no longer rippled with each step.
A cluster of pigeons settled calmly instead of being bounced skyward.

Mossback Morning, who almost never stopped before midday watering time, was slowing to a halt.

The Keepers exchanged one look.
Then they moved.

Tavi and Jun hurried for the route board to announce a gentle pause.
Imani and Suri went to the balance tanks to make sure all water loads stayed even if the city stood still on a slope.
Niko ran to Lantern Market shouting, "Soft stop! Soft stop! Please secure your soup!"

It was an excellent warning, though it caused one dumpling seller to laugh so hard she nearly forgot the soup part.

Within minutes, Turtleshell City shifted from bustling morning to calm careful stillness.
Wagon brakes were set.
Bridge latches were checked.
Potted trees were tied against sudden sway.
Window boxes were brought in from outer ledges.
Children were asked, kindly but firmly, not to use the railing paths as pretend airships until further notice.

Then the Keepers met again at the ridge.

"Balance is good," said Suri.

"Markets are calm," said Niko. "One cabbage rolled away, but it has been recovered."

"North water holds steady," said Imani.

"And the bell is louder on the lower ring," said Jun, tapping their tablet map. "Listen."

They followed Jun down a spiral stair tucked between herb gardens and solar mirrors to a maintenance passage built right into the outer shell band.
This was one of the hidden places ordinary visitors rarely saw.
The walls were smooth green-brown shell, polished by years of careful hands. Copper gauges winked softly. Pipes carried fresh water, warm water, gray water, and herb-steam for the turtle baths. Every few yards, little painted signs reminded the crew:

Tighten kindly.
Walk softly.
Listen twice.

The bell sounded again.

Dong.

This time the sound came with a tiny shiver through the wall.

Imani pressed both palms against the shell.
"It's coming from below the warm-water line."

"Bathworks?" said Niko.

The bathworks were one of the most beloved and most important systems in the whole city.
At rest stops, the Keepers used them to send warm water and soothing herbal rinse through special channels that flowed down along parts of Mossback Morning's shell edge and neck harness. It helped wash away dust, cool tired skin, and keep the great turtle comfortable on long journeys.

If something in the bathworks had jammed, leaked, or rung loose, it could bother Mossback Morning badly.

They followed the passage until they reached a round iron hatch painted with a smiling turtle and a warning in gold letters:
Bathworks Lower Chamber. Mind the steam.

Inside, the chamber smelled of rosemary, mint, cedar, and warm stone.
Copper pipes looped overhead like giant shining vines.
Valves ticked. Gauges trembled. And in the very center stood the old pressure bell.

Everyone stared.

The pressure bell was real, after all.
It hung above the main soothing-water boiler, green with age and polished only around the edges where generations of Keepers had touched it.

Jun squinted.
"I thought this was retired."

"It was," said Imani slowly. "A long time ago. Before the whisper gauges."

"Then why is it ringing?" Niko asked.

As if in answer—

Dong.

The bell gave a patient note, and one of the pressure needles crept into a yellow band.
Not danger. Not yet. But not right either.

Tavi knelt beside the base of the boiler.
There, almost hidden behind a bracket, a small braided root had slipped in through a vent grate. It had grown around a release lever and was pulling it only partway back each time the system sighed.

No wonder the bell was ringing.
The bathworks could not settle pressure smoothly.
And if the bathworks were uncomfortable, Mossback Morning might be stopping because he wanted relief.

Suri crouched beside Tavi.
"A root? From where?"

Jun traced the pipe map upward.
"Roof garden seventeen. The mint-and-marigold terraces."

Niko looked delighted despite the emergency.
"We are being attacked by gardening."

"We are being reminded," said Imani, already reaching for her tools, "that every system touches another system."

The root was too tightly wound to yank free.
If they pulled too hard, they might snap the lever.
If they cut in the wrong place, the pipe could shift and release a burst of hot herb-steam.

So the Keepers did what good Keepers always did.
They slowed down.

Imani shut two side valves with careful half-turns.
Suri braced the lever with a padded clamp.
Jun climbed to the gauge rail and counted each pressure tick aloud.
Tavi whispered to the old bell as if it were a patient elder with useful opinions.
And Niko, after offering three bad suggestions and one decent one, held the lantern low so everyone could see.

"A little more," said Imani.
"Steady," said Suri.
"Pressure falling by crumbs," said Jun.
"Good crumbs or worrying crumbs?" Niko asked.
"Helpful crumbs," said Jun.

Tavi eased the root with a bone-handled garden hook, teasing it loose a thread at a time.
At last the root slipped free enough for Imani to guide the lever gently home.

The gauge needle dropped from yellow to green.
The pipes gave one long relieved sigh.

And the bell fell silent.

For one breathless moment, nothing happened.

Then—
through the listening tubes in the passage wall—
the shell-song returned to its full calm rhythm.

Breath, heart, step, hush.
Breath, heart, step, hush.

Above them, a distant cheer drifted down from the streets.
The whole city had felt it.
Mossback Morning was comfortable again.

Niko threw both hands in the air.
"We fixed the turtle!"

Imani raised an eyebrow.
"We helped the turtle feel better. There is a difference."

"A very important difference," said Tavi, smiling.

But they were all smiling now.
Even Jun, whose smiles often arrived disguised as smaller frowns.

The work was not quite done, of course.
Good maintenance never ended with the exciting part.
They still had to trace the root path, repair the vent grate, trim the terrace bed properly, and update the maintenance notes so future Keepers would know the old pressure bell still worked and still deserved respect.

So up they went, following the sneaky root all the way to roof garden seventeen.
There they found Mrs. Alda, the terrace gardener, kneeling among mint blossoms with her sleeves rolled up and her silver braid pinned in a loop around her head.

When she heard what had happened, she pressed both hands to her cheeks.
"My goodness. I knew that mint was ambitious, but I did not realize it had unionized."

Niko laughed so hard he nearly tipped into a marigold tub.

Together they lifted the terrace boxes, guided the root back where it belonged, and fitted a new fine-mesh guard over the vent. Mrs. Alda promised to keep the mint trimmed. Jun added a red star to the route board for weekly garden-adjacent pipe checks. Suri retied the rain harness. Imani polished the old bell until it glowed softly in the dim chamber. Tavi wrote a careful note in the city log:

When the hidden bell rings, do not panic.
Something is asking to be noticed kindly.

By late afternoon, Mossback Morning had started walking again.
Slowly at first.
Then with his usual deep and peaceful rhythm.
The whole city swayed with relief.
Lantern Market reopened. Soup resumed. Children were once again permitted to pretend the railing paths were airships, provided at least one hand stayed on the rope rail.

That evening, the Keepers sat together on the west bridge as the sky turned apricot and violet.
Far below, the great turtle's head moved through tall grass as gently as a boat through reeds.
A flock of white birds lifted from the plain and circled once around the city like drifting confetti.

Niko bit into a honey bun and looked thoughtful.
"Do you think Mossback Morning knows we fixed the bell?"

Tavi rested their chin on their knees.
"I think he knows we listened."

As if he had heard, the whole bridge gave the tiniest warm tremor.
Not a jolt.
Not a stumble.
Just one deep comfortable step.

The lanterns along the rail chimed together.

Imani smiled into the evening wind.
"That's turtle for thank you," she said.

Nobody argued with her.

Below the bridge, the hidden mechanisms hummed. Above it, the first stars appeared.
And Turtleshell City walked on through the wide kind world, carried by a giant gentle turtle and watched over by the Keepers who loved their moving home with all their careful hearts.
