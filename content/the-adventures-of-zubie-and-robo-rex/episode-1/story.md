# The Adventures of Zubie and Robo-Rex

## Episode 1
**Title:** The Broken Toy That Fixed Itself
**Voice:** EN - Jane, Curious
**Length Target:** ~9 minutes

Zubie and Robo-Rex were best friends.

Not the sort of best friends who only played together sometimes.
Not the sort of best friends who forgot each other when a new toy rolled by.
Zubie and Robo-Rex were the every-day kind of best friends.
The blanket-fort kind.
The cereal-breakfast kind.
The race-across-the-living-room kind.
The whisper-right-before-bed kind.

Robo-Rex was small enough to fit under Zubie’s arm and blue as a bright piece of sky. He had round little robot feet, a tail that wiggled when he beeped, and green eyes that glowed like tiny night-lights.

At bedtime, Zubie tucked himself under his covers in his rocket pajamas, laid Robo-Rex beside his pillow, and pressed the silver button on Robo-Rex’s back.

Usually, Robo-Rex answered right away.
Beep-beep.
A soft happy whirr.
Maybe a little click of his head.
Maybe one proud robot-dinosaur stomp across the blanket.

But that night, nothing happened.

Zubie pressed the button again.
Still nothing.

He sat up.
“Robo-Rex?”

Nothing.
No beep.
No whirr.
No glow in the green eyes.

Zubie’s room suddenly felt too quiet.
The lamp on his dresser still made the walls golden.
His rocket blanket was still soft.
The stars on his curtains still glimmered faintly in the light from the hallway.
But something important was missing.

He picked Robo-Rex up carefully and gave him the gentlest shake.
“Come on, buddy.”

Robo-Rex stayed quiet.

Zubie’s dad came to the doorway just then, wearing a charcoal hoodie and carrying the warm, calm look he always had at bedtime.
“Everything okay in here?” he asked.

Zubie looked down at the toy in his hands.
“Robo-Rex stopped.”

Dad crossed the room and sat on the edge of the bed.
He took Robo-Rex gently, turned him over, pressed the button once, then twice, and listened.

“Hmmm,” Dad said.

That was not a bad sound exactly.
But it was not the sound Zubie wanted.

Dad checked the battery flap.
He looked at one tiny wheel on Robo-Rex’s foot.
He tapped the side lightly and tried the button again.
Nothing.

“Can you fix him?” Zubie asked.

Dad gave him the honest kind of smile.
“I can try.”

So Dad tried.
He looked closely.
He turned Robo-Rex this way and that.
He even set him on the blanket and gave the silver button one more hopeful press.

Nothing.

Zubie’s shoulders drooped.
He leaned forward until his curls almost brushed Robo-Rex’s little robot nose.
“I wish some magic would fix you while I’m sleeping,” he whispered.

Dad’s face softened.
“That’s a pretty good wish,” he said.

He tucked Zubie in again, set Robo-Rex beside the pillow, and kissed the top of Zubie’s head.
“Let’s get some sleep,” he said. “Morning can surprise us sometimes.”

Zubie wanted to believe that.
He really did.
But as he closed his eyes, he kept one hand resting on Robo-Rex’s cool blue back.
Just in case a tiny beep came back.

That night Zubie dreamed of bright robot stars and silver dino footprints across the moon.
In the dream, every footprint made a soft beep, and every beep sounded like laughter.

Then warm morning light slipped across his room.
A bird chirped outside.
Somewhere in the kitchen, something clinked softly.
And right beside Zubie’s ear, a cheerful sound said:

Beep-beep!

Zubie’s eyes flew open.

Robo-Rex’s green eyes were glowing.
His tail gave a wiggle.
He made his tiny proud stomp right across Zubie’s blanket like nothing had ever been wrong.

Zubie gasped, then laughed so hard he almost tangled himself in the covers.
“Robo-Rex!”

Beep-beep!

Now this was very strange.
Because last night Robo-Rex had been very much broken.
And this morning Robo-Rex was very much not broken.

Zubie scooped him up and squeezed him close.
Then he stopped.
His eyes narrowed in the thoughtful way they always did when his brain started doing important work.

Because something felt mysterious.
Very mysterious.

He carried Robo-Rex into the hallway and discovered the first clue almost at once.
Dad’s toolbox was sitting open on the floor near the laundry closet.
It was not usually left open.
Dad liked things neat.
Very neat.

Zubie crouched beside it.
Inside were screwdrivers, pliers, tape, and a little flashlight.
One screwdriver was lying sideways as if it had been put down in a hurry.

Zubie looked over one shoulder.
Nobody in sight.

He whispered, “Interesting.”

That was exactly what detectives said in mysteries.

He tiptoed to the kitchen, where Dad was flipping pancakes.
Dad looked up and smiled.
“Good morning.”

Zubie hugged Robo-Rex tighter.
“Good morning,” he said.
Then he studied Dad’s face very carefully.
Dad looked normal.
Too normal, maybe.

Zubie took a bite of pancake.
Then he hurried back to his room to continue the investigation.

There, near the leg of his bed, he found clue number two.
A tiny silver screw.
Just one.
Small as a sprinkle.
Bright as a fish scale.

Zubie picked it up between his fingers.
He was absolutely sure that screw had not been there last night.

“Very interesting,” he whispered.

He set the screw on his bedside table and sniffed the air.
At first he smelled syrup from breakfast.
Then blanket.
Then crayons from the cup on his shelf.
And then—

Wait.
What was that?

A funny sharp smell.
Not bad.
Just... sticky.
Like when art projects dried on construction paper.
Like when something had been fixed.

Glue.

Zubie sniffed again around Robo-Rex’s side.
There it was.
A tiny glue smell.
Very tiny.
But definitely there.

Now Zubie’s eyes got even wider.
He sat down cross-legged on the rug with Robo-Rex in front of him.
“Toolbox,” he said softly. “Tiny screw. Glue smell.”

This was becoming a serious case.

He needed one more clue.
Great detectives always found one more clue.

So he looked carefully at Robo-Rex from nose to tail.
Blue head.
Blue arms.
Blue dino knees.
And there—right near one side of Robo-Rex’s battery flap—was the faintest little silver smudge.

It looked like someone had touched the toy with careful hands after holding a metal tool.

Zubie sat back.
He folded his arms.
He thought and thought and thought.

Then he grabbed a little notepad from his drawing desk and a green crayon.
On the top page he wrote, as neatly as a six-year-old detective could:

THE CASE OF THE BROKEN TOY THAT FIXED ITSELF

Under that, he drew four clues:
- open toolbox
- tiny screw
- glue smell
- silver smudge

Then he tapped the crayon against his chin.

Last night, Robo-Rex had stopped working.
Dad had tried to help.
Zubie had wished for magic.
This morning, Robo-Rex worked again.

But toys did not usually fix themselves.
Not even excellent robot dinosaurs.

Slowly, very slowly, a smile spread across Zubie’s face.

“Ohhh,” he said.

This was not a mystery anymore.
This was a solved mystery.

Detective Zubie picked up Robo-Rex and marched to the kitchen.
Dad was stacking pancakes onto a plate.
Sunlight came through the window and made everything look soft and bright.

Zubie stood there for a second, holding his toy and his notepad.
Then he asked, “Dad... did you fix Robo-Rex?”

Dad looked at the toy.
Then at the notepad.
Then at Zubie’s very serious detective face.

A slow smile reached his eyes.

“What makes you think that?” he asked.

Zubie held up one finger.
“Open toolbox.”
Another finger.
“Tiny screw.”
Another.
“Glue smell.”
Another.
“Silver smudge.”

Dad laughed softly, the way people do when they are surprised and proud at the same time.

“You noticed all that?”

Zubie nodded.
“I’m a detective.”

Dad lowered himself so they were face to face.
“Well, Detective Zubie,” he said, “I think you solved it.”

“You fixed him?”

Dad nodded.
“One little piece inside had come loose. I found it after you fell asleep. It took a tiny screwdriver, a steady hand, and just a little repair glue.”

Zubie looked down at Robo-Rex.
Robo-Rex blinked his green eyes and gave a happy beep, as if he agreed with the whole report.

“But I wished for magic,” Zubie said.

Dad’s voice turned quiet and warm.
“Sometimes,” he said, “the best kind of magic is made with love... and a little screwdriver.”

For a moment Zubie just looked at him.
Then he threw his arms around Dad in a giant pancake-smelling hug.

“You’re better than magic,” Zubie said.

Dad hugged him back.
Robo-Rex got squished between them and answered with a bright cheerful series of beeps.
Beep-beep! Beep-beep-beep!

That made both of them laugh.

After breakfast, Zubie and Robo-Rex built a blanket cave in the living room.
Then they explored the couch mountains.
Then they guarded the hallway from invisible stompy meteors.
And every time Robo-Rex beeped, Zubie smiled a little bigger.

Because now he knew something important.
Something true.
Something worth remembering.

Magic was wonderful in stories.
But in his house, there was another kind of magic too.
A kind with patient hands.
A kind that stayed up late.
A kind that noticed what mattered.
A kind that loved you enough to fix what was hurting.

And that, Detective Zubie decided, was the very best kind of magic of all.

Sometimes the best kind of magic is made with love... and a little screwdriver.
