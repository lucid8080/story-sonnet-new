## Final Read Script
Blocky Explores Mine World.
Episode one: Blocky Builds a Fort with 1 by 1 Blocks.

In Mine World, mornings didn’t begin with birds.
They began with plinks, clacks, and the cheerful thump of blocks being stacked somewhere nearby.

And on this particular morning, Blocky woke up with a brilliant idea.

I’m going to build a fort, Blocky announced.

Peb looked up from sorting square stone tiles.
A normal fort? Peb asked.

No, said Blocky proudly.
A legendary fort.

Zip zipped in through the doorway so fast that three tiny wood blocks bounced out of a basket.
With launch ramps? Zip asked.

Glow popped up from behind a pile of shiny copper cubes.
With treasure windows?

Moss peered through the doorway carrying a little tray of seed biscuits.
With snack shelves? Moss asked hopefully.

Blocky grinned.
With all of it.

Then Blocky lifted one tiny block between two fingers.

But I’m only using one by one blocks.

Peb blinked slowly.
That, Peb said, sounds like a terrible structural decision.

Blocky waved a hand.
It will be magnificent. Tiny blocks mean tiny precision. Tiny precision means greatness.

Zip nodded very seriously.
That is definitely how greatness works.

So the whole group hurried out to the Build Patch, a wide sunny square at the edge of town where the ground was flat, the sky was bright, and ambitious ideas often became either wonderful inventions or funny accidents.

Blocky planted a little flag in the center of the patch.
Here, Blocky said, we build Fort Blockington.

Glow gasped.
It already sounds expensive.

Not expensive, said Blocky.
Legendary.

The plan started simply enough.
A front wall.
Two side walls.
A lookout tower.
A treasure room.
A snack shelf.
A secret escape tunnel.
A roof garden.
A rainwater chute.
And, for reasons Blocky could not fully explain, a chimney.

Peb stared at the sketch.
You are building a fort, Peb said carefully, not a tiny kingdom.

Why choose? Blocky replied.

They got to work.

Blocky stacked one-by-one stone blocks as fast as possible.
Zip built ladders in places that did not obviously need ladders.
Glow arranged bright glass cubes into treasure windows that glimmered like jewels.
Moss added mossy corners, soft leaf benches, and the very important snack shelf.
Peb, meanwhile, walked around squinting at everything.

That wall leans, Peb said.
That corner wobbles.
That tower is pretending to be straight.
That chimney is attached to nothing.

It is attached, said Blocky, to vision.

Vision, Peb replied, is not load-bearing.

By lunchtime, the fort was amazing to look at.
It had tiny windows, tiny doors, tiny stairs, two flags, three ladders, and one very dramatic roofline.

It was also a complete wobble-box.

When Blocky climbed the lookout tower, the entire upper level gave a soft tick-tick-shiver.
When Zip ran across the front steps, the left wall quivered.
When Glow leaned a golden pebble onto a windowsill, the sill fell off politely and landed in the grass.

Moss handed everyone seed biscuits.
Well, Moss said, it is very welcoming.

Peb folded their arms.
It is welcoming collapse.

Blocky sat on an upside-down block crate and frowned at the fort.
It looks right, Blocky said.
So why doesn’t it work right?

Peb sat beside Blocky.
Because you built it fast and fancy before you built it strong, Peb said.

Just then, a distant whirling sound rolled over the Build Patch.

Wrrr-rrr-rrr-rrr.

High above the hills, little gray cubes were spinning through the sky in a tight zig-zag swirl.
Leaves lifted.
Dust skipped across the ground.
Zip’s scarf flapped sideways.

A cube-storm, Moss said.
A windy one, Peb added.

A windy cube-storm? said Glow. That sounds extra rude.

Blocky stared at Fort Blockington.
Fort Blockington stared shakily back.

It will be fine, Blocky said.

One flag snapped off and flew into a berry bush.

It may, said Blocky, be less fine than I hoped.

The first gust hit.
Fwump.

The treasure window rattled.
The lookout tower leaned.
The chimney, which had indeed been attached mostly to vision, tipped over and landed in the snack shelf.

My biscuits! cried Moss.

Everybody stabilize! shouted Blocky.

The problem was that no one had the same idea of what stabilizing meant.

Zip started adding more ladders.
Glow tried to brace the treasure room with shiny decorative blocks.
Moss rescued the biscuits.
Peb grabbed the fort sketch and began crossing things out at terrifying speed.

No roof garden, Peb said.
No second flag. No treasure window ledge. No decorative mini-arches. No chimney of dreams.

You can’t remove the mini-arches, Blocky said. They’re extremely fort-like.

The fort has to survive the next three minutes, Peb said. Then it can be extremely fort-like later.

Another gust hit.
Fwump-whip.

The right wall rippled.
The tiny door spun in place like it was reconsidering its profession.

Blocky took a deep breath.
Then another.

This was the part Blocky liked least: the moment when a brilliant idea had to be changed.
But Blocky also did not want their brilliant idea to blow into six different biomes.

So Blocky stood on the wobbling front step and called out,
New plan.

Everyone froze.

We stop building the fanciest fort, Blocky said.
We build the strongest small fort we can, right now.

Peb gave a sharp nod.
Now they were talking.

Blocky pointed quickly.
Peb, tell us what stays.
Glow, only use strong blocks.
Zip, no more ladders unless we truly, deeply need a ladder.
Moss, save the snack shelf and help anchor the corners.

Excellent, said Moss, gathering the biscuits in both arms.

They moved fast.

Peb chose four strong corner points and had everyone reinforce them first.
Block by block.
Low and steady.
No nonsense.

Glow swapped the glittery window edges for thick gold-speckled support cubes.
Zip muttered sadly about the loss of launch ramps but helped brace the doorway.
Moss packed the base with sturdy earth blocks and tucked leaf cushions safely inside the finished part.

Blocky, now listening very hard, rebuilt the tower shorter and wider.

No one ever sings songs about a short tower, Blocky muttered.

They do, Peb said. They just survive long enough to finish the song.

Gust after gust swept across the Build Patch.
Fwump.
Rattle.
Whip.
Swish.

But this time the fort did not panic.
It held.

The doorway stayed straight.
The corners stayed grounded.
The small tower stood firm.
The roof, now simple and sturdy, did not wobble even once.

At last the cube-storm rolled away toward the far hills.
The air calmed.
The grass settled.
Zip’s scarf drooped back into place.

Everyone stared at the fort.

It was smaller now.
Simpler.
Less dramatic.
The treasure window was gone.
The second flag was gone.
The chimney had retired permanently.

But the fort stood proud and solid in the sun.

Blocky walked slowly around it.
Pressed the wall.
Climbed the tower.
Stomped the little front platform once, just to check.

Nothing shook.

It works, Blocky said softly.

Glow smiled.
It really does.

Moss put the snack shelf back inside.
In my opinion, Moss said, that means it is a complete success.

Zip looked around.
You know what it needs now?

Peb raised one eyebrow.
If you say launch ramp—

A name plaque, Zip said.

That, said Peb, is actually acceptable.

So they made a small sign from polished oak blocks and set it above the door.

It read:
Fort Blockington.
Built Tiny. Built Strong.

Blocky read it twice.
Then grinned.

That, Blocky said, is better than legendary.

Then Glow had an idea.
If the fort is strong now, Glow said, maybe it can still be beautiful too.

So, with the storm gone and the structure steady, they added only the details that made sense.
A neat little treasure niche built safely into the wall.
A properly supported window.
A biscuit shelf that did not collapse under emotional pressure.
One flag.
Just one.
And a square flower box Moss insisted made the whole place friendlier.

By sunset, Fort Blockington was finished.
Not huge.
Not wild.
Not ridiculous.

Well.
Not very ridiculous.

It was the kind of fort that felt safe when you stepped inside.
The kind of fort that welcomed friends.
The kind of fort that could survive a windy day and still have room for biscuits.

That evening, the whole group sat inside the fort and watched the sky turn orange, then pink, then deep pixel-blue.
Glow tucked one shiny cube into the treasure niche.
Zip leaned against the wall without knocking it down.
Moss passed around the last seed biscuits.
Peb inspected the corners one final time and finally looked satisfied.

Blocky sat in the middle of it all and smiled.

I think, Blocky said, I learned something important.

Peb nodded.
Yes. Chimneys should not be attached to vision.

Everyone laughed.
Even Blocky.

Okay, said Blocky. I learned two important things.

The fort held their laughter easily.
The flag fluttered once in the evening breeze.
And high above the Build Patch, the first square star blinked into the sky as if Mine World itself approved.

The end.
