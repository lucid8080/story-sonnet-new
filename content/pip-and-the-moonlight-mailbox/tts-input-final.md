# Pip and the Moonlight Mailbox — Final TTS Input Pack

## Voice Settings
- Voice: EN - Jane, Curious
- Audience: ages 4–6
- Delivery style: warm, playful, cozy, gentle bedtime energy
- Pace: slightly slow and expressive, but still natural
- Avoid: overly dramatic intensity, horror-style tension, robotic flatness, rushed reading

## Performance Notes
- Read like a caring storyteller sitting beside a child at bedtime
- Keep wonder and softness in the voice
- Give tiny pauses after short standalone lines like “A tiny blue mailbox.”
- Different character voices should be subtle, not theatrical
- Hazel should sound sleepy and cozy
- Flicker should sound small and worried, then brighter
- The cloud should sound puffy, nervous, and then relieved
- Keep Pip curious, kind, and gently brave throughout

## Final Read Script
Pip and the Moonlight Mailbox.
Episode one: The Letter for the Bravest Good Helper.
For ages four to six.

Pip lived in a little yellow house with a creaky garden gate, a patch of mint by the steps, and the softest nighttime breeze in the whole neighborhood.

Every evening, Pip brushed their teeth, put on moon-and-star pajamas, and peeked outside just in case something interesting had decided to happen.

Most nights, the garden looked ordinary.
A sleepy fence.
A round birdbath.
A row of daisies nodding like they were almost asleep.

But on the brightest moon nights, something magical appeared at the end of the garden path.

A tiny blue mailbox.

It was never there in the daytime.
Only at night.
Only in moonlight.
And only when the world felt quiet enough to hear a secret.

Pip had seen it twice before, glowing softly like it had swallowed a little star. But tonight, the mailbox had its tiny silver door popped open.

Inside was a letter.

On the front, written in swirly silver ink, were the words:
To the Bravest Good Helper Nearby.

Pip looked over one shoulder.
Then the other.
The garden was empty.

Bravest good helper? Pip whispered.
That must be... me?

The letter gave a polite little shimmer, as if to say,
Yes. Obviously.

Pip opened it carefully.

Inside was a moon-white note.

Dear Pip,
Three important letters must be delivered before sunrise.
One to a sleepy hedgehog.
One to a nervous firefly.
One to a little cloud that has forgotten how to rain gently.
Please hurry. The moon is waiting.

At the bottom was a silver map no bigger than Pip’s hand.
It sparkled with three glowing dots.

Pip took a deep breath.
This was definitely bigger than finding a lost sock.
This was maybe even bigger than feeding the neighbor’s goldfish.

But the night was soft.
The letter felt warm.
And somebody needed help.

So Pip nodded.
All right, they said. I can do brave. I think. Probably. Tiny brave still counts.

The mailbox gave a pleased little ding.

Inside were three envelopes.
One smelled faintly like blackberries.
One blinked with tiny gold specks.
One was cool and misty, like the air right before rain.

Pip tucked the letters into a satchel, slipped on rain boots, and stepped through the moonlit gate.

The first glowing dot on the map led to Bramble Hollow, where hedgehogs liked to nap under blackberry bushes.

The path wiggled past the birdbath, behind the daisies, and through a patch of tall grass that whispered against Pip’s boots.

Soon Pip heard a sound.
Snrrrff.
Snrrrff.
Snuffle-snore.

There, curled into a prickly little comma under the blackberry leaves, was Hazel the hedgehog. She was wearing a lavender sleep cap with one floppy pom-pom.

Hazel? Pip whispered.

Hazel did not move.

Pip tried again.
Hazel? I have moon mail.

One shiny black nose twitched.
One eye blinked open.

Moon mail? Hazel mumbled. At this hour? It had better be cozy.

Pip handed her the blackberry-scented envelope.
Hazel opened it and read:

Dear Hazel,
Thank you for keeping the bramble paths neat and beetle-safe.
Tonight the moon awards you one extra excellent nap.

Hazel put a paw over her heart.
Why, she said, that is cozy.

Then she reached under a leaf and pulled out a tiny lantern made from a dew drop and a button.

For your journey, Hazel said. The next path is dim.

Pip clipped the lantern to the satchel.
Its soft light bobbed like a friendly firefly.

The second glowing dot led toward the old stump at the edge of the garden, where the grasses grew tall and the air buzzed faintly.

That was where Pip found Flicker.

Flicker was a firefly, but he was not flashing.
He sat on a mushroom looking worried.

Hello, Pip said gently. Are you Flicker?

Maybe, said the firefly in a tiny voice. I used to be. But tonight my glow feels all wobbly. What if I blink at the wrong time? What if everyone else twinkles perfectly and I go plink instead of blink?

Pip sat down on the moss nearby.
That sounds hard, they said.

Flicker nodded sadly.
It is hard. I practiced three times and worried six times.

That is a lot of worrying, Pip agreed. Good thing I brought moon mail.

Flicker opened the gold-speckled envelope.
Inside, the note read:

Dear Flicker,
The night does not need perfect light.
It needs your light.
Small glows count.
Wobbly glows count.
Brave little blinks count too.

Flicker read it twice.
Then a tiny dot of golden light appeared at the tip of his tail.

Blink.
Then another.
Blink-blink.

Soon he was glowing like a warm star tucked into a teacup.

It worked! cried Flicker.

He zipped in a happy loop around Pip’s head, then hovered still.

Take this, he said.
He shook loose a pinch of glow dust into Pip’s palm. It sparkled like cheerful sugar.
If the last delivery gets gloomy, toss the dust into the air. It reminds things how to shine.

Pip thanked him and checked the map.
Only one glowing dot remained.

It floated above the tallest hill beyond the garden, where the sky felt close enough to tap.

Pip followed the winding path uphill.
The grass turned silver in the moonlight.
Crickets chirped.
The lantern glowed.
And overhead, a small gray cloud drifted in nervous little circles.

It sniffled.

Pip climbed onto the hill and called up,
Hello! Are you the cloud who forgot how to rain gently?

The cloud drooped lower.
Yes, it said in a puffy, trembly voice. I used to make soft rainy sounds on windows and patter nicely in puddles. But last week I got startled by thunder, and now every time I try to rain, I do this.

The cloud squeezed its eyes shut.

Splosh!

A giant splash of water tumbled down, soaking the hill, the grass, and one of Pip’s boots.

Oh, said Pip.
That is... not very gentle.

I know! wailed the cloud. I am too much cloud. I should probably just float away forever.

Pip’s tummy fluttered.
This was the part where brave felt a little bigger than tiny.

But then Pip remembered Hazel’s lantern.
And Flicker’s note.
And the way the mailbox had trusted them.

So Pip stood up straighter, even in a soggy boot.

Wait, Pip said. You do not have to be perfect on the first try. You might just need help remembering.

They held up the cool misty envelope.
The cloud carefully opened it.

Inside was a note written in pale blue ink:

Dear Little Cloud,
Rain is not a crash.
Rain is a song.
Start with one drop.
Then another.
Listen to the leaves.
They will teach you the rhythm.

The cloud blinked.
One drop?

One drop, Pip said.

The cloud tried.

Plip.

A single raindrop landed on a blade of grass.
The grass gave a happy shiver.

The cloud tried again.

Plip.
Plip.
Plip-plip.

Soon a soft silver rain began to fall.
Not a crash.
Not a splosh.
A patter.
A whisper.
A little song on leaves.

The hill smelled fresh and green.
The cloud gave a surprised laugh.

I did it, it said. I did it gently.

Pip grinned so hard their cheeks felt moon-round.
You did.

The cloud drifted lower and wrapped Pip in the faintest, fluffiest mist-hug.

For helping me remember, it said, here is a rain pearl.

A tiny shining drop landed in Pip’s hand, cool and bright as crystal.

At that very moment, the silver map glowed all over.
The three dots twinkled, then swirled together into one bright arrow pointing home.

Pip hurried back down the hill, through the tall grass, past the old stump, through Bramble Hollow, and into the garden just as the sky began turning from black to blueberry.

The tiny blue mailbox waited at the end of the path.
Its door stood open.

Inside was one final note.

Dear Pip,
All three letters delivered.
All three hearts helped.
This is what brave looks like.
Not loud.
Not giant.
Just kind enough to keep going.

Pip read the note once.
Then twice.
Then smiled the quiet sort of smile that feels warm all the way down to your toes.

Because maybe bravery was not always a roar.
Maybe sometimes it was moon pajamas, soggy boots, and carrying kindness through the dark.

Pip placed Hazel’s lantern, Flicker’s glow dust, and the rain pearl inside the mailbox for safekeeping.
The box gave a soft happy hum.
Then, as the first stripe of sunrise stretched over the fence, it faded away.

Gone.
As if it had never been there at all.

Except...
On the garden path, right where the mailbox had stood, was a new silver word glowing for just a second before the morning light melted it away.

Soon.

Pip looked up at the brightening sky and giggled.

All right, they said. I will keep my boots ready.

And from somewhere far above, where the moon was tucking itself to bed, a small voice seemed to whisper:
Good helper.

The end.
