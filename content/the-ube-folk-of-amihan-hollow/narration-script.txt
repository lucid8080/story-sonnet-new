The Ube Folk of Amihan Hollow.
The Glow Root Relay.

The morning mist in Amihan Hollow always smelled a little bit sweet.
Not candy-sweet.
Not flower-sweet.
Root-sweet.
Earthy, cozy, purple-y sweet.

That was because the hills of Amihan Hollow were full of ube.

Ube is a purple yam.
It grows underground in big knobby roots.
When people cook it, it can be soft and creamy and gently sweet.
In some kitchens it becomes cakes or jam or pastries or ice cream.
In Amihan Hollow, it became all of those things, and a little bit more.

Because the ube in Amihan Hollow was magical.

Not every root glowed.
Not every leaf sparkled.
Only the best-loved rows did that.
The ones planted with patient hands.
The ones watered at dawn.
The ones sung to during weeding.
The ones thanked before harvest.

Those roots held moonlight.

And the little purple people of Amihan Hollow knew exactly how to care for them.

If you had seen the Ube Folk from far away, you would not have mistaken them for anybody else.
They were shaped like tiny pear drops standing upright, with broad soft middles, short springy legs, and long balancing feet made for slick terraces and fast games.
Their leaf-sail ears tipped outward in the breeze like small green pennants.
Most wore woven sash-belts, seed-pocket vests, or fluttering ponchos stitched from river grass cloth.
When they ran, their ankle ribbons streamed behind them like festival banners.

On the highest terrace that morning, Mina stood with her hands on her hips and squinted at the sky.
Mina wore a fan-leaf visor the color of plum skin and a notebook tucked into her sash.
She liked lists, plans, patterns, and winning relay races in exactly that order.

Below her, the whole village was already awake.
Loop baskets thumped softly against backs.
Water channels chuckled over polished stone.
Steam puffed from cooking huts where purple buns were rising in bamboo trays.
Somebody was roasting coconut.
Somebody was mashing warm ube with milk.
Somebody was definitely sneaking a spoonful before breakfast was served.

That somebody was Bembem.

Bembem looked up from a clay bowl with a lavender moustache over his grin.
I am taste-testing for village safety, he announced.

You are stealing again with extra words, Mina called down.

Toto laughed so hard he nearly dropped a whole bundle of practice batons.
Toto was the strongest digger in Amihan Hollow and built like a happy little drum.
His seed-pocket vest bulged with twine, snacks, and one smooth lucky stone he insisted improved every sport.

Ilya came skipping across the stepping stones with silver ribbons tied around both ankles.
She was the fastest hand-to-hand passer in the Hollow and could catch a relay baton without even looking straight at it.
At least, that was what she said.
What was true was that she usually caught it.

Today was the day of the Glow Root Relay.

Once every turning season, the Ube Folk of Amihan Hollow played a grand match against the people of Taro Village.
It was not exactly soccer.
It was not exactly racing.
It was not exactly harvest work either.
It was a little bit of all three.

Each team had to run a winding course through terraces, over bamboo bridges, around rain jars, and across a soft field lane.
Instead of passing a plain stick, they passed a smooth carved harvest wand.
At the end, the final runner had to place the wand beside a festival basket without knocking over the ceremonial roots stacked there.
Fast feet mattered.
Good balance mattered.
Gentle hands mattered too.

Apo Sela said that was why the relay was such a good game.
It teaches the body to hurry, she always said, without teaching the heart to shove.

Apo Sela was already standing near the moonwater cistern, her shawl tied neatly over one shoulder and her basket hook tucked against her back.
She was one of the oldest growers in the Hollow.
She knew how to tell a thirsty terrace by the sound of wind over the leaves.
She knew which roots were ready by how the soil cracked in tiny smiling lines.
She also knew when children were pretending not to listen.

Come here, relay feet, she called.
Before games, we greet the roots.

So Mina, Toto, Ilya, and Bembem hurried over, trying to look respectful and not like people who wanted breakfast first.

Apo Sela knelt beside a mound where broad vine leaves spread over dark, rich soil.
Very carefully, she brushed away some dirt and lifted one ube partway from the earth.
It was deep purple, almost velvet-dark, with a soft silver glow humming through its skin.

Everyone went quiet.

Glow root, whispered Ilya.

Apo Sela nodded.
Only a few this season. That is enough. Magic is not better when it is bigger. It is better when it is cared for.

Bembem leaned closer.
What will this one do?

Depends on the root, the cook, and the need, said Apo Sela.
Some keep lanterns burning.
Some help tired soil wake back up.
Some hold warmth on rainy nights.
Some brighten memory.

Toto widened his eyes.
Can one make me run faster?

Apo Sela gave him a look over the top of her spectacles.
Practice makes you run faster.
Breakfast helps.
Good sleep helps.
A glow root is not a shortcut for laziness.

Mina smothered a laugh.

Apo Sela set the root back gently and covered it with soil.
Remember this, all of you.
Ube is food.
Ube is work.
Ube is celebration.
In our Hollow, ube is also magic.
That is why we protect it.

She looked toward the far path that wound out of the valley.
Not everyone who comes smiling understands gratitude.

Mina followed her gaze.
At the edge of the lower terraces, three strangers were pretending to study the festival flags.
They wore dusty travel cloaks and carried empty sacks rolled under their arms.
One of them pointed, not at the flags, not at the relay lane, but at the glow-root rows.

Mina’s ears tipped sharply.
Apo, she murmured. Do you know them?

Apo Sela’s face stayed calm.
No. And I do not like the look of their shoes.

This was a very Apo Sela way to judge danger.
She believed shoes told the truth about a person’s intentions.
These shoes were narrow, hard-soled, and not at all shaped for muddy terraces.
They were the shoes of people who did not expect to stay and help.

I’ll tell the watch posts, Mina said.

You will tell no one in a panic, said Apo Sela.
Today is a festival day. We use clear heads. Bring me your team after breakfast.

Breakfast was ube porridge with coconut curls and slices of banana, plus a soft purple roll still warm from the steamer.
It should have been impossible to worry while eating something so comforting.
Mina worried anyway.

Across the river, the people of Taro Village were arriving with laughing voices and striped field scarves.
They came in long river canoes and on the stepping bridge, carrying drums, picnic baskets, and their own relay wands wrapped in cloth.
Their captain, Sani, jumped out first and waved both arms.
Sani wore a taro-leaf capelet and had the kind of steady grin that made every contest feel more exciting instead of more scary.

Ready to lose politely? Sani shouted.

Mina cupped her hands.
Only if you’re ready to win politely.

That was how the two villages talked before matches.
Big grins.
Big jokes.
No meanness.
The Taro Village Striders were rivals, but good ones.
They trained hard, helped reset fallen markers, and never laughed when someone tripped unless the someone laughing was themselves.

While musicians tuned their drums, Apo Sela gathered both team captains under the shade of a drying rack.
Mina noticed Sani’s smile fade when the elder pointed toward the strangers.

Root thieves? Sani whispered.

Maybe, said Apo Sela.
Maybe only fools. We will treat them like both until we know.

Sani set their jaw.
The Striders can help.

Mina blinked.
She had expected strategy for winning, not strategy for sharing.
Then again, that was why Taro Village was worth playing against.

The plan was simple.
The festival would continue so the visitors would not know they had been noticed.
But runners from both villages would keep their eyes open during the relay.
The glowing root rows near the east terraces would be watched by hidden gardeners.
And if the strangers moved toward the protected mounds, Mina would blow the little gourd whistle hanging from her sash.

If we stop the race, we stop it, said Apo Sela.
Harvest comes before ribbons.

Mina nodded.
And then the drums began.

The Glow Root Relay always started with a run through singing banners.
Purple and green streamers fluttered above the first lane while both villages clapped in rhythm.
The first runners crouched at the line.
Ilya for Amihan Hollow.
Piko from Taro Village on the opposite lane.

Gentle feet, quick hands, shouted the crowd.

The whistle trilled.

They flew.

Ilya sprang over the first terrace lip so lightly she barely seemed to touch it.
Piko matched her stride for stride over the bamboo slats.
They curved around the rain jars, splashed through a shallow crossing, and raced back toward the passing stone.
When Ilya reached Mina, she slapped the harvest wand neatly into her palm without breaking pace.

Mina ran.

She loved the middle of a relay.
Everything sharpened there.
The drumming.
The wind.
The feel of packed soil under her long balancing feet.
The voices behind her blending into one rolling wave.
She darted under a vine arch, across the pebble lane, and up the terrace steps two at a time.

That was when she saw one of the strangers slip away from the festival edge.

He moved low between two drying racks and vanished toward the glow-root rows.
Another followed.
The third stayed behind, pretending to clap.
Very badly.

Mina almost blew the whistle.
Then she saw Sani, already watching from the next bend.
Sani touched two fingers to their taro-leaf capelet and angled their run toward the east terraces.
They had seen it too.

Mina reached Toto and thrust the wand into his hands.
East rows, she hissed.

Toto did not ask a single question.
He just nodded and thundered off along the course, which in Toto’s case meant running like a happy rolling boulder.

The crowd kept cheering.
The drums kept playing.
Only a handful of runners and gardeners knew the race had suddenly become two things at once.
A game.
And a guard.

From the upper terrace, Bembem saw the strangers clearly now.
They had unfolded their sacks and were crouching by the protected mounds.
One jammed a metal trowel into the soil.
Another whispered, take the shiny ones first.

Bembem’s whole face scrunched.
That was such a rude sentence.

He yanked a coil of ribbon markers from his vest and darted sideways off the lane.
Bembem was not the fastest runner in the village.
He was, however, the best at turning festival supplies into surprises.

The first thief tugged on a glow root.
Nothing happened.
Or rather, something did happen.
The root gave off a bright violet pulse, and every lantern gourd hanging nearby flashed on at once.
The hidden gardeners popped up from behind the mounds with muddy hands and loud voices.
Hoy.

The thieves yelped.
One dropped his trowel.
The other clutched the half-lifted root, which now glowed brighter and brighter until his sack turned see-through purple in the light.
Inside it were hooks, rope, and three stolen mangoes from somewhere else entirely.

Mina blew the whistle.

The festival music stopped.
Then, amazingly, it started again, but faster.
Taro Village’s drummers had switched to the alarm rhythm they used during flood practice.
Not a scary boom.
A quick organizing beat.
Come here. Look sharp. Move together.

Sani vaulted a rain jar and landed in front of the row.
Toto skidded in from the opposite side.
Bembem whipped his ribbon markers low across two bamboo stakes, and suddenly the path behind the thieves turned into a bright crisscross wall of fluttering streamers.
Not strong enough to trap anyone forever.
Strong enough to confuse hurrying feet.

Ilya arrived with three taro runners at her heels.
One of them was carrying the actual relay wand because, somehow, the race had not completely stopped.
It had simply become very strange.

Put the roots down, Mina called.

The thief holding the glow root tried to yank it free.
Bad idea.
Glow roots did not like rude hands.
The soil around the mound puffed upward in a soft violet cloud, coating his cloak, his nose, and both eyebrows in purple dust.
He sneezed three times so hard he sat down by accident.

The other thief tried to run for the bridge.
But he had chosen terrace paths full of slick moonwater channels.
Sani flicked their capelet off and tossed it over the channel like a decoy flag.
The thief jumped the wrong way, landed ankle-deep in mud, and made the surprised noise of a goose learning a new opinion.

The third one finally stopped fake-clapping and bolted.
He made it exactly six steps before Apo Sela stepped into his path with her basket hook.
She did not poke him with it.
She did not need to.
She simply raised one eyebrow.

Shoes told the truth, she said.

The man froze.

All around them, villagers gathered, but not in a shouting, wild way.
In a working way.
Gardeners checked the roots.
Taro runners blocked the path to the river.
Children were moved gently back.
Someone handed the sneezing thief a cloth, which was kinder than he deserved but very Amihan Hollow.

The glow root was settled carefully back into its mound.
The violet light softened.
The lantern gourds dimmed to a warm hum.
Nothing had been broken.
Nothing had been lost.
Except, perhaps, the thieves’ pride.

Why steal from a festival? Mina asked, breathless and angry and shaky all at once.

The least muddy thief looked down.
We heard the purple yam here was worth a fortune, he muttered.
We thought it was just treasure in the ground.

Apo Sela clicked her tongue.
Just treasure? It is breakfast. It is planting for next season. It is dessert for weddings. It is medicine for tired days. It is lantern light in rainy months. It is work shared by many hands. If you only see price, you do not see it at all.

Sani crossed their arms.
Also, you interrupted a relay, which is terrible manners.

Even Mina laughed at that.
The tightness in her chest loosened a little.

The strangers were marched, not roughly, but firmly, to the footbridge watch post, where they would spend the rest of the festival doing the most embarrassing possible punishment: helping clean mud off borrowed tools under supervision while everyone else ate sweets.

When the path was safe again, the crowd looked uncertain.
The race lane stood empty.
The drummers held their sticks up, waiting.
One of the relay judges glanced at Apo Sela.

Apo Sela glanced at Mina.
Then at Sani.
Then at the half-finished course.

Well? she asked.
Were your legs only brave enough for half a relay?

The cheer that went up from both villages could probably be heard all the way to the far taro ponds.

So the Glow Root Relay began again.
Not from the start.
From the handoff where the trouble had interrupted it.
Because work worth doing does not always need to begin all over to be finished well.

This time, when Mina ran, she felt something different beneath the ordinary joy of speed.
Not fear.
Not exactly relief.
Pride, maybe.
Pride that her village had protected the roots.
Pride that Taro Village had stepped in without being asked twice.
Pride that games and gardens could belong together.

The final leg came down to Toto for Amihan Hollow and Sani for Taro Village.
The crowd roared.
The drummers rolled.
Both runners barreled toward the festival basket with their teammates shouting themselves breathless.

Toto had strength.
Sani had astonishing balance.
For three long pounding seconds they were perfectly even.
Then Toto remembered not to stomp the last turn.
Apo Sela was always saying that the final step was won by calm.
He shortened his stride, settled the wand, and slid it beside the basket with one neat tap.

A breath later, Sani did the same on the opposite mat.
So close.
So beautifully close.

The judges measured.
They murmured.
They re-measured.
Then they held up both ribbons.

A tie.

For one surprised beat, nobody moved.
Then both villages exploded into laughter and cheers because, truly, that was the most satisfying answer the day could have chosen.

By sunset the terraces were strung with lantern gourds glowing plum, gold, and soft silver.
Long tables stretched between bamboo posts.
There were taro cakes from across the river and warm ube halaya from the Hollow, sticky rice rolls, coconut curls, and little fried twists dusted with sugar.
Children from both villages raced with harmless dyed-purple tongues.
Adults told the story of the thieves with more and more dramatic sneezing every time.

Mina sat beside Sani on the terrace wall, each of them holding a bowl of chilled ube dessert swirled with coconut cream.
Below them, Apo Sela showed a cluster of younger children how to pat soil gently back over the glow-root mounds.

Do you ever get tired of everyone caring so much about roots? Sani asked.

Mina took a bite and thought about it.
She watched the lantern light settling over the fields.
She watched Toto teaching taro runners a terrible warm-up stretch.
She watched Bembem trying to improve a spoon by tying ribbons to it for no reason at all.

No, she said at last. Because it’s not really only about roots.

Sani nodded.
They both knew.
It was about what people protected together.
Food.
Tradition.
Play.
Neighbors.
The season ahead.

Down in the nearest mound, one hidden glow root gave a quiet little pulse under the soil, as if pleased.
The lantern gourds answered with a softer shine.
The evening breeze lifted the leaf-sail ears of the Ube Folk and carried the smell of sweet yam, river air, and toasted coconut through the Hollow.

And in Amihan Hollow, that smelled exactly like a day worth remembering.
